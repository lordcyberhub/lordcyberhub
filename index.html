Cybersecurity Trends: Problem, Impact and Solution

A cybersecurity trend is defined as a systematic and sustained change in the dynamics of the digital threat landscape. These shifts are typically driven by the widespread adoption of new offensive or defensive technologies, or by fundamental changes in the geopolitical and economic objectives of threat actors. At the time of this presentation, three trends have been outlined, which are: supply chain attacks, AI-powered offensive and defensive capabilities and the persistent cybersecurity skills gap.
1. Supply Chain Attacks (SCA)
• The Problem: Attackers exploit the trust organizations place in their external software vendors. They compromise a common software update (like the 2020 SolarWinds attack) and use this trusted channel to sneak malicious code into your network. This means your organization inherits the security risks of every partner you rely on, making the vendor with the weakest defences your "weakest link."
• The Impact: For Large Companies, this is an effective bypass for their strong perimeter defences. For Small Businesses, the impact can be devastating. Small businesses rely heavily on third-party IT providers, and if the provider is compromised, the SMB loses their only reliable protection, often leading to total business disruption.
• The Solution: Security professionals must implement rigorous Vendor Risk Management (VRM) and demand a Software Bill of Materials. This allows professionals to check for risks beforehand and implement technical controls, such as network segmentation, to prevent a compromised component from spreading trouble laterally across the network.
2. AI-Powered Offensive and Defensive Capabilities
• The Problem: Generative AI lowers the bar for criminals, allowing them to create highly convincing and automated attacks quickly. The core risk is Adversarial AI, where attackers find ways to trick the security industry's own AI-driven defence systems, either by corrupting the training data or crafting inputs that the defence AI cannot identify as malicious. Internally, staff using unsanctioned Shadow AI also creates risks for sensitive data governance.
• The Impact: Small Businesses are overwhelmed by sophisticated, personalized fraud, such as automated Business Email Compromise (BEC) scams that are almost impossible to distinguish from genuine communication. Enterprises must manage the dual challenge of defending their complex, expensive AI security systems from manipulation while enforcing governance over their massive datasets.
• The Solution: Professionals must apply Zero Trust Architecture (ZTA) to AI systems, ensuring access to models and training data is always verified and based on the least privilege principle. Essential skills include running aggressive Adversarial Testing and continuous monitoring (24/7) to proactively validate AI model integrity and ensure defences are resilient against sophisticated, fast-moving attacks.
3. The Persistent Cybersecurity Skills Gap (CSG)
• The Problem: There is a chronic, worldwide shortage of skilled cybersecurity professionals (millions more are needed). This leads directly to excessive workload and burnout for up to 85% of existing security staff, which increases the likelihood of human error and reduces overall vigilance. Financially, organizations with skill gaps are nearly twice as likely to experience a breach, costing them an average of $1.76 million more.
• The Impact: Small Businesses are the hardest hit; they often lack the expertise to simply configure and monitor their basic security tools. As a result, 74% of their ransomware incidents lead to data encryption, a high failure rate. Enterprises face high recruitment costs, slowed innovation, and the continuous loss of institutional knowledge due to high staff turnover driven by burnout.
• The Solution: The best strategy is to use AI-Driven Automation to handle routine, low-level alerts, freeing up human experts to focus on complex, high-impact tasks. Organizations should prioritize Internal Cross-Training by developing existing IT personnel into security roles which is faster, more effective, and builds a more adaptive team than trying to hire from the limited external talent pool.
